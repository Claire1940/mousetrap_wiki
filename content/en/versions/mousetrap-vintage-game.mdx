<Section icon={Sparkles} title="Step-by-Step Assembly Guide">

Assembling the **mousetrap vintage game** is part of the gameplay. Unlike most games where setup happens before play, Mouse Trap requires players to add pieces to the board as they land on specific "Build" spaces. Following the correct order ensures the trap works when the crank is finally turned.

<Steps>
  <Step num="1" title="Base Foundation">
    Place the plastic base plate on the board. Attach the crank and the large gear. Ensure the gear teeth mesh perfectly with the crank mechanism to prevent slipping during the high-tension trigger phase.
  </Step>
  <Step num="2" title="The Vertical Climb">
    Install the rickety stairs and the marble pipe. The stairs must be balanced so that the marble can roll down without causing the entire structure to wobble. This is often where most mechanical failures occur in vintage sets.
  </Step>
  <Step num="3" title="The Boot and Bucket">
    Attach the boot to the pivot arm and place the bucket at the bottom of the chute. The boot must be positioned at a specific angle so that when the marble hits the lever, the boot "kicks" the bucket with enough force to tip the marble into the next chute.
  </Step>
  <Step num="4" title="The Diver and Tub">
    Set the diving board and place the plastic man on the edge. This requires a small rubber band to provide the "spring" action. The tub must be positioned directly below the diver to catch him and trigger the final cage release.
  </Step>
  <Step num="5" title="The Cage Rigging">
    Slide the cage onto the vertical pole and lock it into the trigger arm. Test the trigger manually once before starting the game to ensure the cage drops straight down onto the "Trap" space.
  </Step>
</Steps>

<Callout type="success" title="Assembly Hack">
If the marble keeps falling off the rickety stairs, check if the board is perfectly flat. Vintage boards often warp; placing a small piece of paper under the board corner can level the "physics" of the game.
</Callout>

<Section icon={Swords} title="Gameplay Rules and Winning Strategies">

The **mousetrap vintage game** is won by outlasting your opponents. While the trap is the star of the show, the cheese collection mechanic is what drives the strategy. Players move their mouse pawns around the board, collecting cheese wedges that can be used later to force opponents into the trap zone.

**Core Gameplay Mechanics:**
- **Building**: Land on a numbered space to add that specific part to the trap.
- **Collecting**: Cheese pieces act as "lives" or currency to move opponents.
- **Trapping**: Once the trap is fully built, landing on the "Turn Crank" space allows you to attempt a capture.

<CardGrid cols={2}>
  <Card title="Aggressive Strategy">
    - **Focus on Build spaces** to finish the trap quickly.
    - Stay near the crank to trigger the trap frequently.
    - Use cheese to move opponents onto the Trap space.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Defensive Strategy">
    - **Hoard cheese pieces** to avoid being moved by others.
    - Stay as far away from the Trap space as possible.
    - Wait for others to trigger the trap and fail.
  </Card>
</CardGrid>

The most critical rule in the vintage edition involves the "Trap" space. You can only turn the crank if an opponent is actually on the designated trap square. If the trap fails to trigger (a common occurrence in vintage sets), the opponent escapes, and the turn ends. This adds a layer of "mechanical risk" to the game strategy.

| Action | Result | Cost |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Land on Build** | Add next trap piece | Free |
| **Land on Cheese** | Take 1 or 2 cheese wedges | Free |
| **Turn Crank** | Attempt to catch mouse | Free (if on space) |
| **Move Opponent** | Force mouse to Trap space | 1 Cheese Wedge |

<Callout type="info" title="Rule Variance">
Many families play with "House Rules" regarding trap failures. A common 1990s house rule is that if the trap fails due to a mechanical error, the player who turned the crank must give one cheese piece to the intended victim.
</Callout>

Owning a **mousetrap vintage game** requires a commitment to preservation. The plastics used in the early 90s can become brittle if exposed to direct sunlight or extreme temperature fluctuations. To keep your game in playable condition for another thirty years, follow these maintenance steps.

If you are missing parts, the secondary market is the best resource. Many collectors sell "parts lots" specifically for the 1992 Milton Bradley version. Ensure you are buying the correct color-coded parts, as the 1960s and 1970s versions used different shades of plastic that may not be compatible with the 1992 gear ratios.

<Callout type="warning" title="Safety Warning">
Vintage sets contain small parts and lead-free but potentially brittle plastics. Ensure children under 6 are supervised, as the small steel marble and cheese wedges are significant choking hazards.
</Callout>
